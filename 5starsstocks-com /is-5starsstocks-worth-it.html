6-Month Backtest Results of 5StarsStocks Picks

Quick Summary: This TodayLite article does not publish unverified performance claims. Instead it gives a transparent, reproducible 6-month backtest method to evaluate 5StarsStocks.com picks, shows how to run the test, and provides an example (illustrative only) so you can verify results yourself.

Why a reproducible backtest matters

Platforms often publish performance snapshots without full methodology. A trustworthy backtest must define rules clearly (entry/exit, fees, dividends, slippage, rebalancing) so anyone can reproduce the numbers. Below we give a step-by-step method you can run yourself and an example template.

Backtest objective

Measure the hypothetical performance of top-rated (4 & 5 star) stock picks signalled by 5StarsStocks.com over a continuous 6-month window, using clear, conservative assumptions.

Define the rules (required)

  • Universe: Stocks that received 4 or 5 stars on 5StarsStocks on the signal date.
  • Signal date: The date the stock first appears as 4/5 stars within the 6-month test period.
  • Entry price: Next market open price after the signal date.
  • Exit rule: Hold for 30 calendar days (example), or exit earlier if rating drops to 2 stars — choose one and be consistent.
  • Position sizing: Equal weight per trade (e.g., fixed $ amount or equal % of portfolio).
  • Costs & slippage: Subtract realistic trading cost (e.g., 0.1% roundtrip) and assume 0.2% slippage on entry/exit for conservatism.
  • Dividends & corporate actions: Include dividends if they occur during the holding period; adjust returns for splits.
  • Data sources: Use exchange-level price data (e.g., Yahoo Finance, Alpha Vantage, or your broker) and archive the 5StarsStocks rating snapshots (screenshots or API if available).

Step-by-step backtest process

  1. Gather signals: For each day in your 6-month window, record tickers that show 4/5 stars on 5StarsStocks and the date/time (screenshot or API export for proof).
  2. Record entry price: Take the next market open price (or next available trade price) after the signal.
  3. Apply exit rule: Determine the exit date and record the exit price (close price on exit date or next open).
  4. Calculate per-trade return: (ExitPrice − EntryPrice) / EntryPrice − costs − slippage + dividends (if any).
  5. Aggregate results: Compute average return, median return, win rate (% trades positive), maximum drawdown, and annualized equivalent if desired.
  6. Compare benchmark: Run the same period performance for a benchmark (S&P 500 / relevant index) to contextualize results.
  7. Document everything: Save raw CSVs, timestamps, and screenshots — transparency is essential for credibility.

How to run this backtest yourself (tools & code)

Two practical ways:

Option A — Google Sheets / Excel (no coding)

Metrics to report

  • Average net return per trade
  • Median net return
  • Win rate (% of trades with net > 0)
  • Compound return (if rebalancing or reinvesting)
  • Max drawdown during the test
  • Benchmark comparison (e.g., S&P 500 over same 6 months)

Common pitfalls and how to avoid them

  • Survivorship bias: Don’t omit delisted or suspended stocks — include them in the dataset.
  • Look-ahead bias: Only use information available on the signal date — do not use future data.
  • Overfitting: Avoid tailoring rules to past winners — keep rules fixed before testing.
  • Data quality: Archive original price snapshots and rating screenshots for auditability.

Interpreting results

If your backtest shows outperformance versus the benchmark, ask:

  • Were transaction costs and slippage modeled conservatively?
  • Does outperformance persist across different market conditions?
  • Is the sample size large enough to be statistically meaningful?
